\section{Find $\lambda$ such that three lines intersect}  

Write the lines in a matrix system of equations:

\begin{align*}
\begin{bmatrix}
0 		& 0 		& 1\\
1 		& \lambda 	& \lambda\\
\lambda & 1 		& \lambda
\end{bmatrix} 
\begin{bmatrix}
x\\
y\\
w
\end{bmatrix} = 
\begin{bmatrix}
0\\
0\\
0
\end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}

Given that $w = 0$, we reduce the system of equations to the following:      
\begin{align*}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 		& \lambda 		\\
\lambda & 1 		
\end{bmatrix} 
\begin{bmatrix}
x\\
y
\end{bmatrix} = 
\begin{bmatrix}
0\\
0
\end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}

Set the determinant equal to zero:
\begin{align*}
1-\lambda &= 0 \\
(1-\lambda)(1 + \lambda ) &= 0 \\
\lambda &= -1, 1
\end{align*}

We choose $\lambda = -1$ since in the other case, we do not have three distinct lines. Using this, we compute the point of intersection as follows:

\begin{align*}
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
0 \\ 
0
\end{bmatrix} \times
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
-1 \\ 
-1
\end{bmatrix} = 
\begin{bmatrix}
0 +1\\
1+0\\ 
0+0
\end{bmatrix} =
\begin{bmatrix}
1\\
1\\ 
0
\end{bmatrix} 
\end{align*}
